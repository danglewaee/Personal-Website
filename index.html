I want to build trustworthy AI systems that help people act earlier, better, and with more confidence.

I came to computer science later than many people around me, around the same time AI suddenly felt close and real. What kept me here was not just the technology itself, but the feeling that better systems could genuinely change how people live with uncertainty.

About

I did not come to computer science early. I came to it looking for a way to build trust where decisions are fragile.

The first spark was the rise of AI. It felt like the beginning of a new industrial revolution, but I kept asking the same question: what should this power be used for? My answer kept bringing me back to my father, a government meteorologist in Vietnam, and the look on his face when an important forecast proved wrong. In a country shaped by storms, floods, and drought, bad predictions are not abstract. They change lives.

That question pushed me toward my first internship at the Institute for Science and Technology Innovation, where I worked with large-scale river-basin data tied to water quality, disaster impact, and downstream communities. Later, in An Giang, I met children studying on old computers that looked exactly like the ones that first pulled me toward computing. I saw myself in them. I wanted technology to feel like possibility, not distance.

Building a small water-monitoring system there changed me. It worked well enough to detect problems, but it also taught me a harder lesson: detection is not prevention. If people are going to trust a system enough to act on it, it has to be rigorous, grounded in real data, and strong enough to earn that trust over time. That is why my work keeps circling back to forecasting, planning, infrastructure, and noisy decisions. I am trying to become the kind of builder who can help make those systems more trustworthy.

Foundations

The mission is personal, but the work still has to be rigorous. These are the tools and training underneath it.

I am still early in the journey, and I do not think the answer is to sound finished before I am. What matters more to me is building the technical depth, discipline, and resilience to keep working on hard problems long enough to earn useful trust.

Right now that means strengthening my base in algorithms, machine learning, systems, and applied infrastructure while taking on projects where failure teaches something real.

Beyond Work

Outside engineering, I keep coming back to the same things: reading signals, trusting teammates, and trying to make sense of uncertainty.

Football, detective novels, and spy stories probably look far away from my technical work, but they are not in my head. They all pull me toward the same patterns: incomplete information, pressure, teamwork, and the difference between reacting late and seeing something early enough to matter.

That is part of why stories like Alan Turing and the Bombe stay with me. Not because of mythology, but because wartime codebreaking at Bletchley Park was ultimately about building a system strong enough to turn hidden signals into action people could trust. I keep coming back to that intersection.
