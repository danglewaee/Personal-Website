My journey

I did not come into computer science through a polished path. It began in an old IT lab, became personal through forecasting, and slowly turned into the same question that still drives my work now: what makes a system trustworthy enough to act on before certainty arrives?

Where it really started

The story began in an old IT lab.

My first memory of computing goes back to fourth grade, when I took part in a city-level informatics competition. The room was full of old desktop towers, office software, and the kind of machines where only a few would turn on at all. It was not a polished beginning, but it stayed with me because even those old computers felt like doors into a much larger world.

What gave it real weight came later. My father works in meteorology, and I grew up seeing the way a wrong forecast could stay on his face after he came home. In Vietnam, where storms, floods, and drought are not distant ideas, a bad forecast does more than miss the weather. It weakens trust, changes how people prepare, and shapes what happens next. That was when computing stopped feeling exciting only because it was new. It became tied to something heavier: how much people depend on systems they have to believe in.

What changed

ISTI and An Giang taught me that detection is not enough.

At ISTI, I started to see the technical side of that problem more clearly. I worked on water-resource assessment around the Sai Gon and Dong Nai river basins, processing hydro-meteorological data for MIKE 11 and NAM simulations. It was the first time I saw how much even strong forecasting and hydrological models still depend on the quality, coverage, and timeliness of the data behind them.

In An Giang, that lesson became personal. During a volunteer camp, we realized many children were not only learning with limited educational resources, but also living with unsafe water conditions. What stayed with me most was how much they reminded me of my own past. They were learning on the same kind of old computers I grew up with, and when I saw them playing Fireboy and Watergirl, I felt a strange flashback to my own childhood. It made the gap between my past, their present, and the future they could still grow into feel very small.

That was when I stopped wanting to only understand the problem and started trying to build something for it. I used my own savings to put together a real-time water quality monitoring system with sensors because I wanted to do more than just notice that something was wrong. The system could detect local water-quality issues, but it also revealed a harsher truth: detection is not prevention. When we asked the local authority for time and support to build a larger-scale solution, they said no. At first that felt like failure, but later I realized they may have been right. A system is not useful just because it can detect something. It only matters if people trust it enough to act on it.

What stays with me

I keep returning to signal, trust, and action.

I have always loved detective and spy stories because the important clue is usually buried somewhere in the noise. Sherlock Holmes stayed with me not because he was magically right, but because he stayed calm long enough to rethink the problem when the obvious explanation failed. That habit stayed with me too. If the first explanation breaks, look again. The signal is usually still there somewhere.

I do not think I can fix the whole world. But I do believe a more trustworthy system can help someone hesitate less, guess less, and make a better decision at the moment it matters most.

Maybe that is why I keep getting pulled toward the same kinds of problems: places where the signal is weak, the pressure is real, and the hardest part is not seeing the data, but knowing when to trust it. That is why my work keeps returning to forecasting, anomaly detection, planning engines, and infrastructure. Different domains, same question: how do we build systems people can actually rely on when the noise is high and someone still has to act?
