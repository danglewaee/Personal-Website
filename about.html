My journey

I came to computer science later than many people around me, but once I found the question I wanted to spend my life on, it stopped feeling like a casual interest and started to feel like a direction.

Where it started

AI was the doorway. Forecasting made it personal.

I was not the kind of student who started coding at a very young age and immediately knew exactly where to go. My interest in computer science really started to take shape around the same time AI and ChatGPT became impossible to ignore. For the first time, technology did not feel like a collection of isolated tools. It felt like the beginning of an industrial shift.

But even then, I kept asking myself a more important question: to what end? The answer kept bringing me back home, to Vietnam, and to my father. He works in meteorology. When forecast bulletins were wrong, I could see the weight of that on his face when he came home. In a country shaped by storms, floods, drought, and long memories of disaster, bad forecasts are not abstract mistakes. They affect trust, decisions, and people's safety.

That was the moment AI stopped being exciting only because it was powerful. I started to wonder what it would mean to use better systems, better models, and better data to strengthen forecasting and decision-making in places that actually need them.

The first real step

ISTI showed me what public-interest data work could look like.

My first serious step was interning at the Institute for Science and Technology Innovation. They were working on large-scale data around the Sai Gon and Dong Nai river basins to understand how weather and disasters affect water quality, how river conditions shape nearby communities, and what larger interventions might reduce harm and improve access to safe water.

That mattered to me because it turned my interest into something concrete. I was no longer just admiring technical systems from a distance. I was contributing to work that sat at the intersection of environmental data, public infrastructure, and human consequence. It helped me see that the kinds of systems I care about most are not just intelligent. They need to be useful, legible, and strong enough to support real decisions.

What An Giang taught me

Detection is not prevention. Trust has to be earned.

A different lesson came through League of Coders. In 2023, I read about primary school students in An Giang, a poor rural province in Vietnam, learning IT on old school computers where several children had to share one machine. In one corner of the photo, some of them were playing Fireboy, a game I loved as a child. That image stayed with me. When I eventually met children there during our summer volunteer camp, I saw myself in them immediately. The old desktop computers, the curiosity, the excitement of shaping something on a screen, all of it felt painfully familiar.

That experience gave me two images at once. One was my own past: crowded computer labs, old machines, and the first path that eventually led me toward CS. The other was a possible future: children who might grow up not just consuming technology, but shaping it. I wanted to believe they could do that. I still do.

During that work, we also became more aware of unsafe water conditions in the area. I used my own savings to build a small real-time water monitoring system because I wanted to do more than observe the problem. The system could detect issues. It could not prevent them. When we tried to push toward something larger, the answer from local authorities was effectively no. That failure taught me one of the most important lessons I have learned so far: a useful system needs more than technical correctness. It needs stronger evidence, broader data, and enough trust that people are willing to act on it.

Since then, the line has stayed with me: detection is not prevention. If I want to build systems that matter at scale, I have to build systems people can actually believe in.

What stays with me

I keep returning to signal, patience, and action under uncertainty.

That instinct shows up in what I read and how I work. I have always loved detective and spy fiction because the truth is never handed over in a clean format. The important clue is usually buried somewhere in the noise. Sherlock Holmes stayed with me not because he was magically right, but because he stayed calm long enough to rethink the problem when the obvious explanation failed. That has shaped how I respond under pressure too. When deadlines stack up and the first approach breaks, I do not want to panic. I want to slow down, look again, and find what I missed.

Even football has reinforced that for me. I have followed Manchester United since 2014. Those years have not been a story of easy success. But I still believe in the idea that confidence can be rebuilt, rhythm can return, and good work done consistently can bring a team back. That belief is personal to me too. I have spent enough time being behind to know that progress is rarely instant. What matters is staying in the work long enough to become the kind of person who can carry it.

That is why my projects range across forecasting, anomaly detection, planning engines, and infrastructure but still feel connected in my head. They all come back to the same question: how do we build systems that help people act earlier, better, and with more confidence when the data is messy and the stakes are real? That is the kind of work I want to keep doing.
