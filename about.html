My journey

I did not come into computer science through a polished path. It started in old computer labs in Vietnam, then became personal through forecasting, water, and the question of what people can actually trust when the data is messy.

Where it really started

The story began in an old IT lab.

My first memory of computing goes back to fourth grade, when I took part in a city-level informatics competition. The room was full of old desktop towers, office software, and the kind of machines where only a few would turn on at all. It was a small beginning, but it stayed with me.

What gave it real weight was my father coming home after a wrong weather bulletin. He works in meteorology. In Vietnam, where storms, floods, and drought are not distant ideas, a bad forecast changes what people trust, how they prepare, and what happens next. That was when computing stopped feeling exciting only because it was new.

What changed

ISTI and An Giang taught me that detection is not enough.

At ISTI, I worked on water-resource assessment around the Sai Gon and Dong Nai river basins, processing hydro-meteorological data for MIKE 11 and NAM simulations. It was the first time I saw how much even strong models still depend on weak sensing, missing observations, and brittle workflows.

In An Giang, that lesson became personal. I met children learning on the same kind of old computers I grew up with, and later built a small water monitoring system with my own savings because I wanted to do more than simply notice the problem. The system could detect issues. It could not prevent them. That failure made one belief stick: people need systems they can act on, not just data.

What stays with me

I keep returning to signal, patience, and action.

I have always loved detective and spy stories because the important clue is usually buried somewhere in the noise. Sherlock Holmes stayed with me not because he was magically right, but because he stayed calm long enough to rethink the problem when the obvious explanation failed. That has shaped how I work too.

That is why my projects range across forecasting, anomaly detection, planning engines, and infrastructure but still feel connected in my head. They all come back to the same question: how do we build systems that help people act earlier, better, and with more confidence when the signal matters and the data is messy?
