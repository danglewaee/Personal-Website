My journey

I did not grow up with new machines or a straight path into computer science. What stayed with me from the beginning was the feeling that technology could open a door, and later, the question of what that door should be used for.

Where it really started

Old computer labs came first. Forecasting gave it weight.

My first memory of computing goes back to fourth grade. I was lucky enough to take part in a city-level informatics competition for elementary students. The room was full of old desktop towers, office software, early tools like Pascal and Scratch, and the kind of machines where only a few would turn on at all. It was a small beginning, but it stayed with me.

What stayed with me even more was what came later: my father coming home after a wrong weather bulletin. He works in meteorology. In Vietnam, where storms, floods, and drought are not distant ideas, a bad forecast is not just a technical miss. It changes what people trust, how they prepare, and what happens next.

That was when computing stopped feeling interesting only because it was new. I started to wonder whether better systems, better data, and eventually AI could strengthen forecasting in places like home. Somewhere in the back of my mind, I kept the same hope: maybe one day my father would smile more often.

The first real step

ISTI showed me how hard the real problem actually is.

My first serious step was interning at the Institute for Science and Technology Innovation. I worked on water-resource assessment around the Sai Gon and Dong Nai river basins, processing hydro-meteorological data for MIKE 11 and NAM simulations. For the first time, I was close to the kind of pipeline that tries to turn environmental measurements into something people can actually plan around.

It also made one thing obvious: even strong models depend on the quality, coverage, and timeliness of the data beneath them. Better algorithms matter, but they do not erase weak sensing, missing observations, or brittle workflows. That realization has stayed with me ever since.

ISTI mattered because it turned an emotional question into a technical one. I was no longer admiring systems from a distance. I was starting to understand what forecasting, monitoring, and public-interest infrastructure actually demand in practice.

What An Giang gave back to me

It brought my own beginning back to me, then taught me what technology still cannot do.

A different lesson came through League of Coders. In 2023, I read about primary school students in An Giang, a poor rural province in Vietnam, learning IT on old school computers where several children had to share one machine. In one corner of the photo, some of them were playing Fireboy, a game that had been part of my own childhood. That image stayed with me long before I met them.

When I eventually met those children during our summer volunteer camp, I felt two images at once. One was my own past: crowded computer labs, old machines, and the first path that quietly led me toward CS. The other was a possible future: children who might grow up not just consuming technology, but shaping it. I saw myself in them because they were curious in the same way I once was, standing in front of the same kind of old machine and imagining something larger than the room they were in.

During that work, we also became more aware of unsafe water conditions in the area. I used my own savings to build a small real-time water monitoring system because I wanted to do more than simply notice the problem. The system could detect issues. It could not prevent them. When we tried to push toward something larger, the answer from local authorities was effectively no. That failure taught me one of the most important lessons I have learned so far: a useful system needs more than technical correctness. It needs stronger evidence, broader data, and enough trust that people are willing to act on it.

Since then, one line has stayed with me: detection is not prevention. If I want to build systems that matter at scale, I have to build systems people can actually believe in.

What stays with me

I keep returning to signal, patience, and action under pressure.

That instinct shows up in what I read and how I work. I have always loved detective and spy fiction because the truth is never handed over in a clean format. The important clue is usually buried somewhere in the noise. Sherlock Holmes stayed with me not because he was magically right, but because he stayed calm long enough to rethink the problem when the obvious explanation failed. That has shaped how I respond under pressure too. When deadlines stack up and the first approach breaks, I do not want to panic. I want to slow down, look again, and find what I missed.

Even football has reinforced that for me. I have followed Manchester United since 2014. Those years have not been a story of easy success. But I still believe in the idea that confidence can be rebuilt, rhythm can return, and good work done consistently can bring a team back. That belief is personal to me too. I have spent enough time being behind to know that progress is rarely instant. What matters is staying in the work long enough to become the kind of person who can carry it.

That is why my projects range across forecasting, anomaly detection, planning engines, and infrastructure but still feel connected in my head. They all come back to the same question: how do we build systems that help people act earlier, better, and with more confidence when the data is messy and the stakes are real? That is the kind of work I am trying to grow into at UMass, and the kind of work I want to keep doing for a long time.
